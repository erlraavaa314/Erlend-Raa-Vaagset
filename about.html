Who I Am

I’m Erlend Raa Vågset, an Assistant Professor at HVL in Førde, Norway. My work focuses on computational complexity and topology, exploring the boundaries of what is computationally possible and how mathematical structures influence computation. Recently, I’ve begun connecting these ideas to robotics, delving into configuration spaces and Lie theory to address interdisciplinary challenges.

Research Interests

My research centers on computational complexity, with a focus on parameterized algorithms for NP-hard problems. I’m particularly interested in how structural properties, such as topological invariants, can simplify challenging computational tasks.

Recently, I’ve begun exploring robotics as a new avenue for applying mathematical concepts. This includes investigating motion planning and how abstract structures like configuration spaces influence practical computation.

Teaching and Projects

At HVL, I teach courses in algorithms, programming, and machine learning. I believe in hands-on, student-active learning that helps students develop confidence and curiosity in computer science. My teaching is guided by the principle that understanding comes from doing, and I strive to create an inclusive and engaging learning environment.

I’m also involved in projects that explore new ways of teaching and bridging computational theory with real-world applications, such as robotics and machine learning.

Academic Background

My academic path began with a Bachelors degree in mathematics, followed by a Masters degree in (algebraic) topology, culminating in a thesis on log Hochschild homology, supervised by Christian Schlichtkrull. These projects introduced me to the algebraic and topological frameworks that continue to shape my work today.

During my PhD at the University of Bergen, I developed several parameterized algorithms for NP-hard problems in topology, supervised by Nello Blaser and Michael Fellows. This work combined ideas from fine-grained complexity and computational topology, laying the foundation for much of my current research. Collaborative projects with researchers at Oregon State University and beyond further enriched this period.

On a Personal Note

When I’m not working, I like trying out new hobbies—some stick, some don’t. Over the years, I’ve spent time kayaking, hiking, cross-country skiing, and even experimenting with home-brewing. I enjoy staying active and finding ways to break up the routine, whether it’s in the outdoors or at home.
