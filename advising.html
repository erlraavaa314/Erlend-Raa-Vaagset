Mentoring and Supervision

I see mentoring as a partnership grounded in trust, dialogue, and care. Rather than relying on strict frameworks, I work closely with students through open conversations that help them develop a deeper understanding of their own thinking, writing, and projects.

My goal is to guide students toward independence — not by removing obstacles, but by helping them navigate challenges with clarity and confidence. I strive to create a space where it feels safe to take risks, sit with complexity, and grow through difficulty. I'm present, attentive, and on their side, without rushing to fix or rescue.

I believe that good mentorship involves knowing when to step in and when to step back. My role is to support, not to shield — to be a steady presence that students can rely on, especially when things get hard, while still leaving room for them to find their own way forward.

I take mentoring seriously because I know both how powerful good mentorship can be — and how painful it is when that support is missing. Earlier in my academic journey, I had a difficult experience with an advisor who failed to offer the care and respect I now see as essential.1 That awareness has shaped a mentoring approach rooted in presence, patience, and genuine collaboration.

1 This refers to an early advising experience and does not include any of the mentors I’ve named elsewhere in these pages. On the contrary, they are all brilliant, generous advisors, and I continually strive to emulate their example in my own work.
