Robotics

Since coming to Førde, I’ve found myself thinking more and more about robotics. The environment here — the people, the labs, the way robotics quietly weaves through the place — has started to shape the kinds of questions I’m asking.

My background is in topology and complexity theory, but it turns out many core robotics problems live right at that intersection. Questions about motion, reachability, safety — these are often questions about the structure of space. And when those spaces are high-dimensional, with constraints and obstacles, topology becomes more than relevant — it becomes necessary.

Configuration Spaces

A configuration space is the set of all ways a robot can exist — its positions, orientations, joint angles, etc. For simple systems, these might be smooth and compact. But in most real-world scenarios, they’re fragmented, strange, full of holes. They’re topological spaces in disguise.

My previous work focused on understanding spaces like this from a computational point of view: how to model them, reason about their structure, and identify what makes them hard. That lens carries over naturally into robotics — especially when we care not just about existence, but about what’s possible, what’s efficient, and what’s fundamentally out of reach.

Path Planning

In robotics, path planning usually means finding a safe, efficient route from one state to another. But once you look at this in the configuration space sense, it becomes something else entirely. It’s no longer about a robot navigating a room — it’s about navigating a high-dimensional space, where each point represents a full-body pose or system state, and where collisions and constraints carve out complex holes and barriers.

From that perspective, planning a path means finding a continuous trajectory through a strange and often fragmented space. Are two configurations connected? What does a bottleneck look like? Are there distinct homotopy classes of solutions? These are topological questions, and they map directly onto things I've worked on: connectivity, complexity, and structure in hard spaces.

The hope is that by applying insights from computational topology and parameterized complexity, we can get better at understanding not just whether a path exists, but why — and what makes one problem easy and another impossibly hard.
